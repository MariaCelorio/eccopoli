Human Rights / Rule of Law

What is the rule of law?

According to the United Nations:

In 1945 the United Nations was created based on three pillars: Peace and international security, human rights and development. Almost seventy-five years later, the complex political, social, and economic transformation of modern society has brought us challenges and opportunities, which require a collective response that must be guided by the rule of law, since it is the foundation of equitable and friendly relationships between states and the basis of fair societies.

For the United Nations (UN) system, the rule of law is a principle of governance in which all persons, institutions and entities, public and private, including the state itself, are accountable to laws that are publicly promulgated, equally applied and independently adjudicated, and which are consistent with international human rights norms and standards. It needs measures to ensure adherence to the principles of supremacy of the law, equality before the law, responsibility before the law, justice in the application of the law, separation of powers, participation in decision-making, legal certainty, avoidance of arbitrariness, and legal and due-process transparency.

The rule of law is fundamental for international peace and security and for political stability; to achieve social and economic progress and development; and to protect fundamental freedoms and people's rights. It is essential for people's access to public services, to divert corruption, to stop the abuse of power, and to establish the social contract between people and the state. The rule of law and development are strongly intertwined, and a strengthened society based on the rule of law should be considered as a result of the 2030 Agenda and the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

(The above based on the United Nations definition)

Human Rights for Eccopoli

For Eccopoli, Human Rights cannot be enforced unless there is a solid rule of law in the communities of the countries where we work. That is why in this pillar the two concepts are intertwined in one. We work to promote a solid rule of law in the communities of the countries in which we work so that then, human rights and women's rights can prevail and be respected above all. However, this applies to all laws.

Through community-level conferences, training for judges and other public officials, Eccopoli makes an impact at the local level. Community members, mayors, police representatives, and civil society, including local NGOs, are summoned to initiate a dialogue on crime and law enforcement in the community. Gaps are identified and programs are implemented, which are designed in collaboration with the authorities in question. Thus, step by step, a decrease in crime is achieved, and to respect the law and promote the rule of law is done.

Human and Social Rights are also an important part of this pillar. Work is being done to ensure that complaints of human and social rights, including ESCR, reach the courts and are properly monitored for their just resolution./p>
